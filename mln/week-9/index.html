MLn CLUB · WEEK 9

From Worm to Human

About this week

We've had the full wiring diagram of a ~300-neuron worm for decades — and now one for a 140,000-neuron fly. So what actually stands between that and emulating the ~86 billion neurons in your head? Today's largest AI clusters are already within striking distance of the raw compute a real-time human- brain emulation would need. If the silicon is nearly there, is the real bottleneck the microscope? From Worm to Human: Scaling Brain Emulation Isaak Freeman (MIT, Boyden Lab) This thesis argues that high-quality, biologically faithful emulation of whole brains — starting with model organisms and aiming eventually at humans — is far more tractable than it sounds, and the case comes from bridging four fields that rarely talk to each other: connectomics, functional imaging, neural simulation, and AI hardware. Connectomics has jumped from the only complete connectome being a 302-neuron roundworm to a full adult fruit-fly brain of roughly 140,000 neurons, while the cost to reconstruct a single neuron has fallen from about $16,500 to roughly $100, and functional imaging now reaches near-complete coverage of larval zebrafish brains and around a million cortical neurons in mice. Translating all of this into common units, the author uses order-of- magnitude estimates to reduce whole-brain emulation to concrete hardware demands — and finds that mid-2020s AI clusters (about 4e20 FLOP/s) are already close to a rough estimate of what a real-time human emulation would need (about 6e20 FLOP/s). Crude human-scale simulations of tens of billions of neurons have already been run, though the work carefully distinguishes a true emulation — one that matches a brain's internal causal dynamics at a chosen level of detail — from these proofs-of-concept, and argues that C. elegans, larval zebrafish, and Drosophila are the compelling near-term targets for building the methods needed to scale toward human brains.
